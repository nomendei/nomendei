Depressive "Realism"

Why bothering?

The last week, during my morning Feedly-consumption routine, I stumbled upon an article with a kind of catchy title: Depressive Realism, on vice.com.

Catchy mainly for me and a few others I guess, as I started myself dealing with depression some years ago. I would like to go through the details of that experience as well, but I prefer to keep this post about the topic discussed in that article: are depressive people more likely to see reality as it truly is?

Reality cannot be simply defined

I have read other articles like this, and it seems that the authors deal with the concept of reality a bit too easily, as if its definition was universally agreed and concrete.

I understand this kind of reasoning in the context of physical matters: one could try to define, for instance, "what a tree really is".
It's a tangible entity, one can feel it with the 5 senses: touch it, smell it, see it, hear it, maybe taste it... A few people in front of this tree could probably agree on some foundamental truth about it, a middle ground which will provide some shared understanding of what it is: it has a hard body, it has soft leaves whose color is different from the body etc.

But what reality really is sounds like a bigger goal to achieve, definitely something that cannot be simply given.

The same goes for other metaphisical concepts, like meaning or death. The linked article suggests that optimism helps "to avoid facing terrifying concepts like death..." and "... helps us power through a life full of heartache, meaninglessness, and death". This is a very narrow, one sided perspective.
We are not dealing with a tree, a physical being. We are dealing with meaning and death, we are exploring the realm of noetic, of very personal and human perception. That's why when we define death as terrifying, we are assuming a one reality equal for all.
"Death is terryfing and that's the ultimate truth".

My psychotherapist once pointed out that "the part of our brain that is actually connected to an external sensor (eyes, ears etc) is less than 20%". What we ultimately perceive as "real" has been 80% processed by the internal network of our brain, which is composed by memories and emotions of the last (in my case) 31 years and 4 months (fake age). So if it's already a small challenge to agree on how a tree is (although we can touch it and smell it and see it etc), how can we possibly agree on a concept that is purely developed and interpreted by our human minds, like meaning and death? Depressive people see these concept in one way, but it's far fetched to think that there is a way more real than another one. Let's consider some different takes on death and meaning, just to make the point.

Death: terryfing or just... death?

"Death is terryfing", many people say. Do we have the same attitude towards our by now well known tree? If a tree is all crooked, do we say "Oh look at that bad ugly tree"? I don't think so. A crooked tree is never considered bad, it's just crooked.
There are plenty of people that accepted death as part of the cycle and they don't judge it. It's not terryfing, it's just death or even simply death. However you consider it (horrible, awesome, bad...), it will anyway remain death.
It won't make any difference if you like it or not. You can live your life thinking about it, which won't help, or accepting it, and carry on with the next quest.
I don't have a thorough knowledge of Buddhism, but I read the Bardo Thodol and the way I interpreted it suggests exactly this point: when your clock stops ticking, you can either embrace death and become Buddha, or you can remain plugged to your karma, be fearful and start the loop over.

Humans and meaning

Some time ago I read a very wonderful book by Viktor E. Frankl: The Feeling of Meaninglessness. I will talk more thoroughly about it on another post. But in a nutshell it goes through the life and work of Viktor Frankl, who created a school of mental treatment called Logotherapy.
It is supposed help people deal with the more human, spiritual level of themselves and help them find meaning in life again. Having a meaning is what makes us human and gives us a directed tension that can drive us through our existence to a purpose.
This, again, is another take on life and meaning. Life does not have to be meaninglessness: as long as we feel we are reaching toward something beyond us, our life if being purposeful. And there has been people working on therapeutic methods to help achieving this.

I am not saying that this is the true interpretation of life, meaning and death. I just wanted to mention an example of a different schools of thought. I don't see why terryfing and meaningless has to be more true than unique and purposeful. There is just the perception of it.

So rather than racing for who has the most real perception of reality, why don't we just try to focus on what brings us to meaining and, consequently, happiness?

Article-ending quote

The one thing you can’t take away from me is the way I choose to respond to what you do to me. The last of one’s freedoms is to choose one’s attitude in any given circumstance.
Viktor E. Frankl, The Feeling of Meaninglessness
